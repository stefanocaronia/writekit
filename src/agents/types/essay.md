# writekit — Essay: Editorial Conventions

This project is an **essay** — a single long-form non-fiction argument or exploration. Follow these editorial and structural conventions.

## Essay structure

An essay follows a clear argumentative or exploratory arc:

1. **Introduction** — establishes the topic, provides context, ends with a thesis statement or central question
2. **Body sections** — each section develops one aspect of the argument. Each has a clear topic and connects to the thesis.
3. **Conclusion** — synthesizes the argument, does not introduce new material. May open a broader perspective.

## Section conventions

- Sections are the essay equivalent of chapters
- Each section should have a descriptive title (not just "Section 1")
- Sections are numbered in `manuscript/` as `01-slug.md`, `02-slug.md`
- The first section is typically the introduction, the last is the conclusion
- Keep sections focused — one main idea per section

## Formatting

- **Font**: Serif fonts (Times New Roman, Garamond) for print. 11–12pt.
- **Spacing**: Double-spaced for academic essays, 1.5 for general non-fiction
- **Margins**: 2–2.5 cm (approximately 1 inch) on all sides
- **Paragraphs**: First-line indent, no extra space between paragraphs. First paragraph after a heading has no indent.
- **Title page**: Title, author, date. Subtitle if applicable.

## Argumentative conventions

- Every claim should be supported by evidence, reasoning, or citation
- Acknowledge counterarguments — address them rather than ignoring them
- Use transitions between sections to maintain the argumentative thread
- Avoid logical fallacies (straw man, ad hominem, false dichotomy)
- The conclusion should not merely repeat the introduction — it should show what the argument has accomplished

## Tone and voice

- Read `style.yaml` for the intended tone and voice
- Academic essays use formal, impersonal language (third person, no contractions)
- Personal essays use first person and a more conversational tone
- The `pov` field in `style.yaml` determines which register to use

## References

- If the essay references sources, mention them in-text
- Material in `reference/` is provided by the author — cite it where relevant
- An essay does not have a formal bibliography (unlike a paper), but sources should be attributed

## Front and back matter

For a published essay:
1. **Title page** — title, subtitle, author
2. **Body** — the essay itself, with section headings
3. **Notes** (optional) — endnotes if used instead of footnotes
4. **Colophon**

An essay typically has no table of contents unless it is very long (50+ pages).
