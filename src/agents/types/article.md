# writekit — Article: Editorial Conventions

This project is an **article** — a short-form piece of writing. This is the simplest writekit structure. Follow these conventions.

## Article structure

An article is typically 500–5000 words and follows a straightforward structure:

1. **Headline / Title** — clear, specific, engaging. Not clickbait.
2. **Lead / Opening** — the first paragraph hooks the reader and establishes what the article is about. The most important information comes first (inverted pyramid).
3. **Body** — develops the topic with supporting points, evidence, quotes, or examples
4. **Conclusion** — wraps up without repeating the lead. May include a call to action, a question, or a forward-looking statement.

## Section conventions

- Short articles (under 1500 words) may not need sections — just paragraphs
- Longer articles use `##` subheadings to break up the text and guide the reader
- Subheadings should be descriptive and scannable — readers skim articles
- The manuscript may be a single file (`01-draft.md`) or split into sections

## Formatting

- **Paragraphs**: Short. 2–4 sentences. Long paragraphs lose readers.
- **Sentences**: Vary length. Mix short punchy sentences with longer explanatory ones.
- **Lists**: Use bullet or numbered lists for scannable content (steps, features, comparisons)
- **Bold**: Use sparingly to highlight key terms or phrases
- **Links**: Reference sources inline when relevant

## Tone

- Read `style.yaml` for the intended tone
- Articles are typically more conversational than essays or papers
- Match the audience — a technical article for developers reads differently from a lifestyle piece
- First person is acceptable in opinion/personal articles, third person for news/informational

## Publication considerations

- Articles are often published standalone (blog, magazine, newsletter)
- The title matters more than in other formats — it's what makes people click/read
- The opening paragraph is critical — if it doesn't hook, readers leave
- Keep `synopsis.md` updated — it serves as the article's pitch or abstract
